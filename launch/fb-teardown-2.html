02 · The locked look — what's different about their vegetables

This is the thing you flagged: their vegetable design is not the one our older scripts used. The difference is exact and it matters for every prompt.

  • The whole head IS one vegetable — potato, watermelon, broccoli, strawberry, brinjal, guava, onion, bitter-gourd, banana, tomato, cauliflower. The skull and face are the vegetable itself.
  • NO human scalp hair. The vegetable's own natural top is the "hair": the okra/tomato stem-cap, the broccoli florets, the strawberry leaf-crown, the brinjal green calyx, the smooth potato top. Our old model wrongly added a head of human hair — that's the mistake this fixes.
  • Human features are embedded in the veg skin — big, glossy, expressive Pixar eyes, human eyebrows (and a moustache on elders), a soft nose, a human mouth, blush on the cheeks. Emotion lives in the eyes; peaks are teary close-ups.
  • Full, normal human body in real clothing — suits, kurtas, sarees/gowns, overalls, ragged patched clothes. Status is shown by dress (rags → suit signals the rise).
  • 3D Pixar / Disney render, photoreal environments (marble mansions, glass offices, banks, drought fields, village lanes), cinematic lighting, shallow depth of field.
  • One consistent design per character across the whole film — held by using a fixed reference image, not by luck.
How we hold it: render one clean reference portrait per character first, approve it, then reuse it as a Flow "Ingredient" in every scene. That is exactly how our new scripts are built — see Short 1.

03 · The formula (their recipe, in one place)

  • One hero, clear stakes. A poor/honest/loyal underdog vs a richer, prouder character. You know in the first 3 seconds who to root for.
  • Family-money-morality drama. Their proven engines: honesty rewarded · a test of character · cheated then repaid by karma · loyalty wrongly accused then vindicated · rags-to-riches. Emotion carried by situation, not cruelty.
  • A real payoff ending — a reveal or a reversal that lands (the letter, the treasure, the receipt, the promotion). Proper start, proper ending — not a "comment for part 2" cliffhanger.
  • A held teary close-up at the peak = the thumbnail frame.
  • Runtime 30s–90s for most (one ran 2:23); dialogue-led, music bed under everything, first line inside the first second.
  • End on a CTA — a burned-in Follow / Share button, added in post.
One thing we change on purpose: the source videos use Muslim framing (अल्लाह, अब्बा, बरकत). Our channel is neutral / Hindu only — so we keep the exact emotional register and morals but swap the framing (भगवान/ऊपरवाला, पिताजी). This is baked into our QA gate.

What is new in 6 and 7 (test this pair against 1–5). Two changes, both deliberate. (1) Modern topics — 1–5 are village/haveli morality tales; 6 is an online-payment fraud and 7 is phone addiction costing a school year. Same emotional engine (a poor hero, an unfair blow, a complete payoff), today's problems. (2) A cold-open HOOK. Scene X.0 is a 4-second flash-forward lifted out of the middle of the film — the brick falling out of the box, the principal sliding the notice across the desk — played before anything is explained. The film then rewinds and runs forward, and when it reaches that moment again it plays in full with its consequence. That rewind is the only cut in either film; every other join stays continuous. The rival teardown lists "no cold-open hook" as a cause of duds — this pair is how we test the fix.

05 · The new reference reel — decoded second by second

The one you sent on its own (Drive link). 66.7s · 720×1280 · 30fps · about 9 shots. Two brothers split the family home — the rich one on the paved side, the poor one on the mud side, a white line down the middle of frame for the whole film. No narrator, no captions, no on-screen text anywhere, one music bed from first frame to last. It does not use the payoff-reveal recipe of the first five, and that is why it is worth copying separately.

Measured, not eyeballed. The audio was transcribed three times (two model sizes, different settings) and then checked with a voice-activity detector and a spectrogram, because transcription alone invents text over loud music. The result: speech fills 90.1% of the runtime, and the longest pause anywhere in the film is 1.4 seconds. My first pass on this reel read a long silent stretch in the middle — that was a transcription failure, not the film. There is no silence in it to copy; the density is the technique.
  • It opens ON the rupture — second 0. No set-up, no "once upon a time", no flash-forward. The first line of the film is the worst line in it: घर का ये हिस्सा मेरा है… और वो हिस्सा तुम्हारा। You are dropped into the argument already in progress and have to stay to find out what caused it.
  • A physical divider is in almost every frame. A white line on the ground between a mud yard and a paved driveway; the composition is split left/right the whole film. You always know what it is about without a word.
  • The dialogue never stops. ~35 short lines a minute, one every 1.5–2 seconds, most of them 4–7 words. Lines abut with no gap. Nobody makes a speech.
  • THE ENGINE: the two young ones fix it, out loud. Halfway in, the two small ones meet at the line — मुझे तुम्हारी बहुत याद आती है… हम साथ में कितने ख़ुश थे — and they make a plan: तुम अपने पापा को मनाओ, मैं अपने पापा को मनाता हूँ। Then each one goes and works on his own father until the man folds: तुम सही कह रहे हो, मुझे ही माफ़ी माँग लेनी चाहिए। That is the whole machine. Not a plot twist — a five-year-old with an argument the adult cannot answer.
  • Wives set it up, young ones close it. One quiet scene per house first (वो तुम्हारा भाई है।) — it softens the men but does not turn them. The young ones turn them.
  • The ending is at the divider, not in a reveal. Both men come back to the line, apologise over each other, and the line is rubbed out on camera with a foot. Then a long held hug, closing lines spoken over it, running to the last frame. No moral card, no text.

06 · The delivery — the part that is hardest to copy

You asked specifically about dialogue delivery. This is what it actually does — and every one of these is written into the video prompts of Shorts 8–10 as a Delivery line, scene by scene.

  • Short lines. Four to seven words. Nobody makes a speech. Most lines run barely over a second — one breath, then stop.
  • No dead air, ever. Lines follow each other immediately; the film never pauses to admire itself. If a rendered clip comes back with a silent tail, trim it.
  • They never overlap. Fast, but strictly one mouth at a time — every line lands before the next starts.
  • Raised, never screaming. The anger is cold and flat. The cruellest line in the film is said quietly.
  • The home scenes drop right down. Half-volume, slower, eyes down or turned away. The wives never accuse — they state.
  • The young ones are fast, small and unafraid. No pleading tone, no performance — they just say the true thing, twice, and wait. That is why it works.
  • The apology is deliberately messy. Each man starts the instant the other stops, both insisting the fault was his.
  • No narrator, ever. Every line comes out of a mouth on screen, in-clip. There is no voice-over track in the whole film.
What we do NOT take: their plot (brothers dividing an inherited house), their cast (potato / brinjal / turnip / beetroot) and their framing. Shorts 8–10 use our own rifts, our own vegetable casts and neutral/Hindu framing — only the machine is copied: open on the rupture · a divider in frame · wall-to-wall short lines · the young ones broker the peace · apology at the divider · the divider destroyed on camera · a long held embrace.

Two Stoves

The Wall in the Middle

बीच की दीवार

A capsicum builds a new house on his father's plot and lays a half-height brick wall across the courtyard: you there, we here.
