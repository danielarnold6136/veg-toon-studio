Two brothers run the one vegetable shop their father left them. After a money quarrel the elder nails plastic sheeting down the middle of the counter and divides it out loud — the scale to one side, the weights to the other. Neither half can weigh anything alone. That night Nimbu and Matar meet at the sheeting, settle it between themselves in five lines, and each goes to work on their own father. The nails come out before the market opens.

Why a scroller stops: A man hammers a nail through the middle of his own father's shop and tells his brother the shop is now half and half — and his brother doesn't argue. You stay because you can already see neither half can work alone.

Rupture: the sheeting goes up, second 0→The split: scale mine, weights yours→Her side: he is your elder brother→His side: I said it in anger→The plan: five lines at the sheeting→She works on her father: I want to see Nimbu bhaiya→He works on his father: the elder makes the first move→The apology: both reach the sheeting together→Payoff: the nails come out
The model this film copies — the new reference reel, not Shorts 1–7. Four things are different on purpose. (1) It opens ON the rupture — second 0 is the worst moment (plastic sheeting nailed down the middle of their father's shop), with no set-up and no flash-forward; the film simply keeps running from there. (2) The dialogue never stops. Measured off the reference audio, 90% of its runtime is speech and the longest pause anywhere in it is 1.4 seconds. So this script carries 40 lines in 68 seconds (~35 a minute) — short lines, back to back, no dead air. (3) The young ones fix it, out loud. The pivot is not an adult and not a plot device. In this film: the two young students meet at the sheeting the same night, agree in five lines who will talk to whom, and then each one goes and works on their own father. That is the reference reel's actual engine. (4) It ends at the divider — the apology is spoken on either side of it, the divider is physically destroyed on camera, and the last shot is a long, held embrace with nobody speaking.
Built for continuity — it must play as ONE unbroken film, not clips. There is no cut anywhere in this film — not even at the hook, because the hook is the first minute of the story itself. Two things hold that: (1) the story stays in a few connected sets and the camera flows between beats (walk / pan / push) instead of cutting — each scene’s Flow line says how it joins the one before. (2) when you render, take the LAST frame of each finished clip and use it as the input / first image for the next clip (Flow → “Frames to Video” / Extend); every video prompt below also carries a continuity lock.
Same locked look as Shorts 1–7: whole-vegetable head with NO human hair (the veg’s own top is the crown) · render the reference portraits first and reuse them as Flow Ingredients · every dialogue line spoken inside its own clip (🎤 one mouth moving at a time — no separate VO recording) · neutral/Hindu framing · everyday India.
Delivery is half the film — read the Delivery line in every scene. The reference reel wins on how the lines are said. The rules it follows, and this script with it: short lines, one breath each, 3–6 words, never a speech; a new line roughly every 1.5–2 seconds; the split lines land hard and clipped, each one finishing before the next starts; the home scenes drop to low, slow and half-turned away; the young students speak fast, small and unafraid — no pleading, no crying, just sentences the adult cannot answer; and the apology is rushed and broken, each one starting the instant the other stops. Only the very last frames are silent, held on the embrace.
