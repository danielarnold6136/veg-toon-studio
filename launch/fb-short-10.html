Shimla builds a new house at the front of his father's plot and lays a half-height brick wall across the courtyard, cutting the old back room off from the family side. Mooli Bapu only asks whether the lane door still opens, and never argues again. That night his grandson Chunnu carries the plate over the wall, holds an umbrella over it through the rain, then goes to his own father — and falls asleep against the bricks holding his grandfather's hand. By morning the wall is coming off.

Why a scroller stops: A son lays a brick wall across his own father's courtyard and tells him: you there, we here. The old man just asks whether the lane door still opens — and it is his grandson, not him, who takes the wall down.

Rupture: the last brick, second 0→The split: the house is in my name→Her side: who takes Bapu his plate?→His side: bricks fall down too→Over the wall: the plate goes across→The rain: take the umbrella, Dada ji→He works on his father: then I'll sleep on that side→The morning: he held my hand all night→The apology: the first brick comes off→Payoff: no wall now
The model this film copies — the new reference reel, not Shorts 1–7. Four things are different on purpose. (1) It opens ON the rupture — second 0 is the worst moment (a half-height brick wall laid across a father's courtyard), with no set-up and no flash-forward; the film simply keeps running from there. (2) The dialogue never stops. Measured off the reference audio, 90% of its runtime is speech and the longest pause anywhere in it is 1.4 seconds. So this script carries 39 lines in 74 seconds (~32 a minute) — short lines, back to back, no dead air. (3) The young ones fix it, out loud. The pivot is not an adult and not a plot device. In this film: there is only one young one here, and he does it alone: he carries the plate over the wall, holds the umbrella over it in the rain, and then puts the argument to his own father in three lines. That is the reference reel's actual engine. (4) It ends at the divider — the apology is spoken on either side of it, the divider is physically destroyed on camera, and the last shot is a long, held embrace with nobody speaking.
Built for continuity — it must play as ONE unbroken film, not clips. There is no cut anywhere in this film — not even at the hook, because the hook is the first minute of the story itself. Two things hold that: (1) the story stays in a few connected sets and the camera flows between beats (walk / pan / push) instead of cutting — each scene’s Flow line says how it joins the one before. (2) when you render, take the LAST frame of each finished clip and use it as the input / first image for the next clip (Flow → “Frames to Video” / Extend); every video prompt below also carries a continuity lock.
Same locked look as Shorts 1–7: whole-vegetable head with NO human hair (the veg’s own top is the crown) · render the reference portraits first and reuse them as Flow Ingredients · every dialogue line spoken inside its own clip (🎤 one mouth moving at a time — no separate VO recording) · neutral/Hindu framing · everyday India.
Delivery is half the film — read the Delivery line in every scene. The reference reel wins on how the lines are said. The rules it follows, and this script with it: short lines, one breath each, 3–6 words, never a speech; a new line roughly every 1.5–2 seconds; the split lines land hard and clipped, each one finishing before the next starts; the home scenes drop to low, slow and half-turned away; the young students speak fast, small and unafraid — no pleading, no crying, just sentences the adult cannot answer; and the apology is rushed and broken, each one starting the instant the other stops. Only the very last frames are silent, held on the embrace.
