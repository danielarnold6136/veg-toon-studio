A vegetable seller has stood in front of the same shop for twenty years. The morning the shop's owner tips his handcart over at the kerb, a crate slides into the open storm drain and jams — and he is told to his face, within ten seconds, that the drain will block. He says it is not his headache. Then it rains.

Why a scroller stops: A man tips a poor vendor's handcart over to clean up his shopfront, is warned on the spot that the drain is now blocked, and waves it away without even looking. The camera holds on the wedged crate just long enough. You already know what the rain is going to do — you watch to find out how long it takes him.

Rupture: get that cart off my front→The crate jams · and he is warned→They move up the lane→The rain — no words, just water→Inside the glass shop: who blocked my drain?→Above the water line: the whole market queues→He looks up the lane. Nobody looks back→Moral part 1: the hero to camera→Moral part 2: narrator over the dark shop
What is NEW in this set (14–15) — read this first. These two are built from a seventh reference reel (~2M views) and three things change from Shorts 1–13. (1) The characters are vegetables all the way down. Up to Short 13 every prompt on this site ended the character block with “the hands are ordinary human hands.” That is now gone. Every piece of exposed skin — hands, fingers, wrists, neck, forearms, ankles, bare feet — is the same colour and same material as that character’s own vegetable head. A tomato has tomato-red hands; a pea has pea-green hands and feet. There is no human skin tone anywhere. (2) The background is a real, deep, sharp set. Every prompt now forbids the empty blurred backdrop and demands densely-layered props all the way into the far distance, so the whole frame reads as one photographed scene. (3) The delivery is visually-led, not wall-to-wall. The reference runs 9 lines in 37.4s = 14.4 lines a minute — less than half the density of Shorts 8–13 — with short lines, a genuinely silent visual stretch, and a two-part moral at the end. Both films here sit close to that envelope — 16.0 and 15.0 lines a minute against the reference’s 14.4, each with one fully silent 8-second shot.
Cuts — this set follows the reference, and it is a change. Shorts 8–13 were built with no cut anywhere. The 2M-view reel is not: it is 5 shots joined by exactly 4 HARD CUTS, with no dissolves and no fades anywhere in the file. So these two films use a hybrid — shots inside the same place and the same minute still flow as one unbroken take (take the LAST frame of the clip as the first image of the next, via Flow → “Frames to Video” / Extend), and every change of place or time is a clean HARD CUT. Each scene is marked either Flow → or HARD CUT, and its video prompt is written accordingly. Short 14 has 5 hard cuts, Short 15 has 6.
Lock the camera off. Measured on the reference: three of its five shots have literally zero camera movement — no pan, no drift, no zoom — held for a full 10 seconds, which AI video models essentially never do by themselves. That stillness is a large part of why it reads as a film rather than as AI footage. Every scene below is therefore a locked-off static tripod shot, except two deliberate eased moves per film (marked on those scenes). If a clip comes back drifting or breathing, re-render it.
The two-part moral — copy this exactly, it is the reference’s signature. The reel does not end on a line of dialogue. It ends on two lines addressed to the viewer as दोस्तो: the hero turns to camera and says the first half in-clip, lip-synced; then a separate narrator voice — measured at a distinctly lower pitch than every character in the film — closes over the final image while no mouth moves at all. Both films below are built that way: scene X.8 is the hero to camera, scene X.9 is picture-only and its two lines are a VO track you record separately and lay on in the edit. Those VO lines are deliberately kept out of the Veo prompt so Veo cannot try to lip-sync them.
