Launch day. The screen above the stage carries one founder portrait, and the man who drew the first page of the product is standing in the audience looking up at it. He does not shout. A junior designer still has the grey sketchbook from the first year, the investor asks one question too many, and by the time the second portrait goes up on that screen it is already too late to matter.

Why a scroller stops: A man on a stage says he built it alone — and the man who actually drew it first is standing in the crowd, saying nothing. You stay to find out whether anybody in that room is going to ask.

Rupture: I built all of this alone→The corridor: then the truth is half too→The sketchbook: the first drawings are yours→The investor: who drew the first sketch?→The page: the drawings answer it→The verdict: I don't buy names→Too late: not a mistake, a choice→Payoff: they walk out, he stays behind the glass
What is NEW in this set (11–13) — read this first. Three things change from Shorts 8–10 and nothing else does. (1) A bright, modern grade. Every image prompt on this page is written for clean high-key daylight, true white balance and a fresh mint / coral / teal / glass palette — and it explicitly bans the amber-yellow, dusty, village-warm look the earlier prompts produced. (2) A modern world and a modern cast. Glass rooftops, café counters, co-working floors, launch halls and duplex kitchens — no village lane anywhere — and a new fruit-and-vegetable cast styled in present-day city clothes (blazers, denim, hoodies, co-ord sets, smartwatches). (3) New angles. 11 is love, 12 is betrayal, 13 is a family fight — instead of the property/inheritance rifts of 8–10. Unchanged on purpose: the delivery model — opens on the rupture at second 0, short lines back to back with no dead air, one mouth moving at a time, and every join a flow instead of a cut.
Built for continuity — it must play as ONE unbroken film, not clips. There is no cut anywhere in this film. Two things hold that: (1) the story stays in a few connected sets and the camera flows between beats (walk / pan / push) instead of cutting — each scene’s Flow line says how it joins the one before. (2) when you render, take the LAST frame of each finished clip and use it as the input / first image for the next clip (Flow → “Frames to Video” / Extend); every video prompt below also carries a continuity lock, and now also a grade lock so Veo cannot drift the bright look back to amber.
Dialogue must read as ONE conversation, not a list of lines. This is the thing Short 8 got right and it is written harder here: every image-to-video prompt below states that each line is a direct answer to the line before it, that it starts the instant the previous line finishes, that the lines never overlap and never leave a gap, and that the whole scene must play as one single continuous exchange rather than separate lines. Each of the three films also hands one sentence across two mouths — one character starts it and the other finishes it, beginning the instant the first stops without ever speaking over them (scenes 11.8, 12.6 and 13.8) — so that seam is impossible to hear. Read the Delivery line in every scene — it is half the film.
Same locked character model as Shorts 1–10: the whole head IS one fruit or vegetable with NO human hair (its own top is the crown) · render the reference portraits first and reuse them as Flow Ingredients · every dialogue line spoken inside its own clip (🎤 one mouth moving at a time — no separate VO recording) · neutral/Hindu framing · no on-screen text ever, in any language, on any screen or label.
