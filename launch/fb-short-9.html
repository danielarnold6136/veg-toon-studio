Two brothers' wives share one kitchen until the morning the elder drags a piece of chalk across the floor and takes her own stove. Pots divided, rations divided, nobody crosses — except the grandmother, who now has no side to sit on and says so in one sentence, then sits down on the line itself. That night two young students tear one roti in half across the chalk and go to work on their mothers, and by first light the line is being wiped off the floor.

Why a scroller stops: A woman drags a chalk line across her own kitchen floor and says the hearth is separate from today — and the old woman standing in the middle of it asks the one question nobody has an answer to: which side do I sit on?

Rupture: the chalk line, second 0→The split: big pot mine, plates mine→No side: which side do I sit on?→The plan: one roti, torn across the chalk→Mirchi works on Tori: Gajar is my sister→Gajar works on Pyaaz: Dadi didn't eat→The apology: both reach for the same cloth→Payoff: one pot, one stove
The model this film copies — the new reference reel, not Shorts 1–7. Four things are different on purpose. (1) It opens ON the rupture — second 0 is the worst moment (a chalk line dragged across a joint family's shared kitchen floor), with no set-up and no flash-forward; the film simply keeps running from there. (2) The dialogue never stops. Measured off the reference audio, 90% of its runtime is speech and the longest pause anywhere in it is 1.4 seconds. So this script carries 33 lines in 58 seconds (~34 a minute) — short lines, back to back, no dead air. (3) The young ones fix it, out loud. The pivot is not an adult and not a plot device. In this film: the two young students split one roti across the chalk, decide in five lines who will speak to whom, and each one argues her own mother down that same night. That is the reference reel's actual engine. (4) It ends at the divider — the apology is spoken on either side of it, the divider is physically destroyed on camera, and the last shot is a long, held embrace with nobody speaking.
Built for continuity — it must play as ONE unbroken film, not clips. There is no cut anywhere in this film — not even at the hook, because the hook is the first minute of the story itself. Two things hold that: (1) the story stays in a few connected sets and the camera flows between beats (walk / pan / push) instead of cutting — each scene’s Flow line says how it joins the one before. (2) when you render, take the LAST frame of each finished clip and use it as the input / first image for the next clip (Flow → “Frames to Video” / Extend); every video prompt below also carries a continuity lock.
Same locked look as Shorts 1–7: whole-vegetable head with NO human hair (the veg’s own top is the crown) · render the reference portraits first and reuse them as Flow Ingredients · every dialogue line spoken inside its own clip (🎤 one mouth moving at a time — no separate VO recording) · neutral/Hindu framing · everyday India.
Delivery is half the film — read the Delivery line in every scene. The reference reel wins on how the lines are said. The rules it follows, and this script with it: short lines, one breath each, 3–6 words, never a speech; a new line roughly every 1.5–2 seconds; the split lines land hard and clipped, each one finishing before the next starts; the home scenes drop to low, slow and half-turned away; the young students speak fast, small and unafraid — no pleading, no crying, just sentences the adult cannot answer; and the apology is rushed and broken, each one starting the instant the other stops. Only the very last frames are silent, held on the embrace.
